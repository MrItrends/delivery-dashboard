# Design Review

## The Review Questions

Before any screen is approved, ask every question. If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure" — revise before shipping.

---

### 1. Does typography carry the hierarchy?

Remove all borders, backgrounds, and color from the screen mentally. Does the information hierarchy remain clear? Can the user still tell what is most important, what is secondary, what is metadata?

If not: the design is relying on decoration to communicate hierarchy that typography should carry.

---

### 2. Can color be reduced further?

Count the distinct colors used in the screen. Can any be replaced with neutral gray without losing meaning? Is any color present purely for visual variety?

If yes: remove it. Color communicates. It should not decorate.

---

### 3. Is the layout quieter than the previous version?

Every revision should be quieter. Not emptier — quieter. Fewer competing elements. More deliberate spacing. Less visual noise.

If the revision added decoration or complexity without a clear reason: undo it.

---

### 4. Can one component be removed?

Scan every element on the screen. Is every component earning its place? Could one section be merged with another? Could one card become a single line in a table?

Add only what the user needs. Remove everything else.

---

### 5. Can navigation become shallower?

Does this screen require the user to navigate away to do something that could happen inline? Can a modal become an inline edit? Can a page become an inspector panel?

Every unnecessary navigation is a context switch. Context switches are expensive.

---

### 6. Can editing happen inline?

Status, owner, priority, due date, title — do these require a separate form or modal? Or can they be edited directly in the table or inspector?

Inline editing is always preferable to navigation.

---

### 7. Does this feel like software people work in for eight hours?

This is the most important question. Not: "Does this look beautiful?" Not: "Would this win a design award?"

**Would a government official be comfortable spending their entire working day in this?**

Beautiful is not the goal. **Comfortable and clear is the goal.**

---

### 8. Would this still look modern in five years?

Is any element on this screen trendy? Glassmorphism, gradient backgrounds, neon accents, rounded blobs, oversized hero sections — these have expiry dates.

Typography-led, whitespace-driven, structurally clear design does not go out of date. Build for timelessness.

---

### 9. Would a Figma designer remove anything from this screen?

Think like the most ruthless editor you know. Not an engineer asking "does it work?" Not a PM asking "does it show the data?"

A world-class product designer asking: "Is there anything here that doesn't need to be here?"

**If yes — remove it.**

---

## The Ten Commandments

These are not guidelines. They are the operating principles of every design decision in the product.

```
I.    Typography before color.
II.   Structure before decoration.
III.  Context before navigation.
IV.   Tables before cards.
V.    Whitespace before borders.
VI.   Reuse before invention.
VII.  Calm before clever.
VIII. Data before dashboards.
IX.   Collaboration before isolation.
X.    Timeless before trendy.
```

When two approaches are in conflict, the commandment resolves it. There is no debate between a card grid and a table. Commandment IV applies.

---

## The Final Manifesto

The Delivery Dashboard should never feel like software generated by AI.

It should feel like software that has matured through years of careful iteration — where every screen has been looked at hundreds of times, where every pixel is where it is for a reason, where nothing is there because someone forgot to remove it.

**Every screen should communicate confidence.**

Not through decoration. Not through clever effects. Through the confidence that comes from knowing exactly what matters and showing only that.

**Every interaction should feel inevitable.**

Users should never think "I wonder how I do this?" They should think "Of course — it's right there." Inevitability is not luck. It is the result of deeply understanding what users need and placing it precisely where they will look.

**Every layout should prioritize information over interface.**

The information is the product. The interface is the delivery mechanism. A delivery mechanism that gets in the way of what it is delivering has failed.

---

## The Three Successes

### Success 1 — The Daily User

> "If a user can spend an entire day inside the application without feeling overwhelmed, the design has succeeded."

Not: "they were impressed the first time they saw it."

The first impression is easy. Designing for the hundredth use is hard. Design for the hundredth use.

### Success 2 — The Executive

> "If a government leader can understand the state of national delivery within minutes, the design has succeeded."

Not: "they can find the data after clicking around."

Within minutes. The hierarchy of information, the visual clarity of status, the instant readability of priorities — these should require no training.

### Success 3 — The Designer

> "If a designer opens the product and cannot immediately identify which framework or template it came from, the design has succeeded."

Not: "they recognize the components."

Cannot identify. The product should feel original. Considered. Built from principles, not borrowed from templates.

---

**This is the standard every future screen, component, interaction, and feature must meet.**
